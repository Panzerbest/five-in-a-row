1. Game Overview

Gomoku (also called Five in a Row, Wuziqi 五子棋, or Gobang) is a two-player abstract strategy game played on a 15×15 board. Black moves first. Players alternate placing a stone of their own color on any empty intersection. The first to line up five stones in a continuous row — horizontally, vertically, or diagonally — wins.

3. Opening Theory

3.1 Straight vs Diagonal

Black's second move decides the opening type:

Straight opening        Diagonal opening
     H                      H
     │                      ╲
  ──●──●──              ──●─╲──
     │                      ●
  • Straight — Black 2 sits on the same row or column as Black 1. Lines stay linear.
  • Diagonal — Black 2 sits diagonally. More variations, more room to grow.

3.2 The 26 Renju Openings

The Renju International Federation (RIF) defines 26 standard openings, named after celestial bodies:

Opening        Black 2 position       Character
───────────────────────────────────────────────
Han (寒星)      straight — adjacent   tight defense
Xi  (溪星)      diagonal — near       balanced
Shu (疏星)      diagonal — far        open, many lines
Hua (花月)      straight              Black-favored, often winning
Pu  (浦月)      straight              aggressive Black attack
Lan (嵐星)      diagonal              defensive White
Yin (银月)      diagonal              balanced
Ming (明星)    straight              open and complex
... 18 more in the official RIF list
Tip for new players: start with Hua-yue if you want to feel attacking, Shu-xing if you want balance.

4. Attacking

4.1 Open Three — the foundation of attack

An open three is the most important attacking shape, because it becomes an open four next move — uncounterable.

Forms of open three:

① Straight:        ·●●●·
   →  Either end completes an open four

② Jump:             ·●·●●·    or   ·●●·●·
   →  Filling the gap turns it into an open four

③ Combined (one stone, two threes):
        ●
        │
   ·─●─·─●─·
        │
        ●
   One move forms two open threes — opponent can only block one!

4.2 Closed Four — never ignore

It looks blockable, but it is the engine of VCF.

Forms of closed four:

① Direct:        ○●●●●·    →  Opponent must block the open end
   or:           ·●●●●○    →  Opponent must block the other end

② Jump:          ●·●●●     →  Opponent must fill the gap (·)

Key idea: a closed four is "absolute initiative"
  You play four → opponent defends → you play four → opponent defends
  Repeat until you set up double-four or four-and-three → win!

VCF example:

  Move 1: four        Move 2: four        Move 3: four + open three
  ──────────         ──────────          ────────────
  ○●·●●·            ○●●●●··             ○●●●●○·
       ↓ fill           ↓ block             ↓
  ○●●●●○            ○●●●●○··             forms open three — win!
  forced block        forced block

4.3 One-stone double threats — the finishing blow

【Double open three】

     ·
     │
  ·─●─·         Black plays the center → a horizontal
     │            AND a vertical open three at once
     ·            White can only block one → other becomes open four → Black wins

【Four + open three】

  ●●●●·         closed four (forced defense)
     +
    ·●●·          open three (also threatening)

  One move creates both → White can't cover both → Black wins

【Double closed four】

  ·●·●● → four     One stone forms two closed fours
     +              White can only block one
  ●→ four            the other becomes five → Black wins

Starting position:

5.2 Where to block an open three

When the opponent has an open three, which end do you block?

Opponent's open three:
  ·○○○·

Where to put your stone — three heuristics:
  ① Block YOUR development side → pick the end that also helps your own stones
  ② Cut their connections    → pick the end that disconnects them from neighbours
  ③ Control a key square     → pick the square they may later need

Example:

     A B C D E F G
  ┌─────────────────┐
5 │ . . ○ . . . . │
4 │ . . . . . . . │
3 │ . ● ○ ○ ○ . . │  ← White's open three
2 │ . . . . . . . │
1 │ . ● . . . . . │
  └─────────────────┘

  Black should block at C3 (the left end):
  ✅ simultaneously connects vertically to B1 and B3
  ✅ cuts off White's space in the lower-left
  ❌ if Black blocked G3 instead, White still has play on the left

5.3 Attack-as-defense (counter-attack)

Pure defense (passive):       Counter-attack (active):

  Opponent open three         Opponent open three
     → you defend                → you play a closed four!
  Opponent open three again    Opponent must answer your four
     → you defend again         You adjust the position in your favour
  ... you're running out       You regain the initiative

  ║  If you can attack, do it!       ║

  ║  initiative — it interrupts the  ║

  ║  defending, pick the most        ║

6.2 The power of the jump

Jumping instead of connecting gives your lines more variations.

Connected vs jumped:

  Straight three   ●●●·        only two extensions
  Jumped three     ●·●·        same three, but more flexibility

  Straight four    ●●●●·       extends only from one end
  Jumped four      ●●·●        the gap fills and you win!

Building from a jump:
  Jumped three ●·●·  →  fill the gap → ●●●· (open three)
  Jumped three ●·●·  →  extend on an end → ●·●●· (also an open three)
  Two paths — harder to read

7.3 One-question decision tree

Q: Where do I play?

  ① Do I have an open four?       → Play it. Win.
  ② Does opponent have open four? → One square only. Block it.
  ③ Do I have four + open three?  → Play the four. Win.
  ④ Does opp have four + open 3 ? → Block the four first, then the three.
  ⑤ Do I have double open three?  → Play it. Win.
  ⑥ Does opp have double open 3 ? → Usually lost. Look for a counter.
  ⑦ Do I have an open three?      → Extend to an open four.
  ⑧ Does opp have open three?     → Block a square that also attacks.
  ⑨ Nothing happening?            → Make a "two" or join existing stones.

8.2 Stone efficiency — one stone, many uses

Inefficient:                     Efficient:

  . ● .     one direction         ╲ │ ╱
  . . .                          . ● .
                                  ╱ │ ╲
                              four directions!

Rule: the more lines a stone belongs to, the more useful it is.
     → prefer intersections where many lines meet
     → center beats corner (more information per stone)

8.3 Building — from shapeless to sharp

"Building" means placing a stone without making an immediate threat, but improving future attacking potential.

Good build:                      Bad build:

  . ● .                          ● . . . .
  . . .         existing stones  . . . . .    lonely group
  . . ● .       in the same line . . . . .    no coordination
                                  . . . .

  Far apart but on the same line   spread out, not talking to each other

8.4 Common mistakes

Mistake 1 — Only watching your own plans
  →  Every move should also check whether the opponent has a hidden kill

Mistake 2 — Random closed fours
  →  Closed fours are ammunition for VCF — spend them only on the line

Mistake 3 — Grudging pure defense
  →  When you're losing, look for the counter-attack; pure defense digs deeper

Mistake 4 — Ignoring diagonals
  →  Four directions are equal: beginners often play only horizontal/vertical,
     then get killed by a diagonal three they didn't notice

9. Rules & Variants

9.1 Renju (professional rules)

To balance Black's first-move advantage, tournament rules restrict Black:

Restriction        What it forbids
───────────────────────────────────────────────
Double-three       Black can't make two open threes
Double-four        Black can't make two fours (incl. closed fours)
Overline           Black can't line up six or more stones
White: none        White has no restrictions

Example — double-three restriction:

      ·
      │
   ·─×─·     Black cannot play × (it would make two open threes)
      │
      ·

White can use these restrictions as a tactic ("forcing-forbidden" play)
to push Black into the forbidden square.

9.2 Swap2 opening

A modern opening used to reduce the first-move advantage:

Flow:
  Step 1: Player A places 3 stones (two Black, one White)
  Step 2: Player B chooses:
    ① Take Black and play the position as-is
    ② Take White and swap colors
    ③ Place 2 more (one Black, one White), then Player A picks color
  Step 3: play normally with the chosen colors

Why it helps: both players can avoid placing an extreme Black-favored shape
             → closer to balanced.

9.3 Pro rules (open-source)

  Black 1 — must be tengen (centre)
  Black 2 and 3 — must land in the central 5×5 region
  After that — no restriction
  Looser than Renju — best for intermediate-to-strong club play.
