How to Play

Five in a Row (also known as Gomoku or Wuziqi) is a classic two-player strategy game played on a 15 × 15 grid. The goal is simple: be the first player to line up five of your stones in an unbroken row — horizontally, vertically, or diagonally.

  • Take turns. White moves first by default, and the computer plays Black. Each turn, place one stone on any empty intersection.
  • Build lines. Stones stay where they are placed — there is no moving or capturing, so winning is about reading the board and planning ahead.
  • Win. The first side to complete five in a row wins the game. Watch out for open fours and double-threes; the AI looks for them, and so should you.
  • Reset. When the game ends, the board clears automatically so you can play again.

Where the Game Comes From

Five in a Row has roots that stretch back more than two thousand years. It grew out of Weiqi (Go), one of the oldest board games still played today. Players in ancient China noticed that lining up five stones on a Go board could be a satisfying contest in its own right, separate from surrounding territory.

The earliest written references appear in Chinese texts from the Tang and Song dynasties (roughly the 7th–13th centuries), where the activity is described alongside other board games of the period. Its modern name, Wuziqi (五子棋, “five-piece chess”), comes from that era.

The game was carried to Japan sometime between the 8th and 12th centuries, where it became known as Gomoku (五目, “five stones”) and developed formal rules. From there it spread westward in the late 19th century under the name “Gobang,” and reached Europe and the Americas as a popular paper-and-pencil pastime before becoming a fixture of online play.
